What a freight salesperson is actually selling

The core idea

A weak freight salesperson thinks they sell transportation. A strong one sells business outcomes: speed to market, inventory continuity, production continuity, launch protection, and execution certainty.

Customers rarely buy air freight because they like airplanes. They buy it because something commercially painful happens if cargo is late, mishandled, or poorly coordinated.

You are not only selling linehaul — you are selling orchestration across pickup, export process, air carriage, customs coordination, and final delivery.

Commercial implications

If you believe you sell only a rate, you will compete on price. If you believe you sell shipment certainty and business protection, you compete on value.

  • When a customer says "Send your best rate," they are often giving a signal, not a fully defined shipment.
  • Your first responsibility is to convert vague demand into a clearly defined movement.

✈ Module 2 of 10
When air freight is the right recommendation

The decision framework

Air freight becomes the right recommendation when the cost of delay exceeds the premium cost of speed.

✅ Strong use cases for air

  • Production protection when downtime is expensive
  • Stockout prevention
  • Launch-date protection (retail, seasonal, promotional)
  • High-value, low-weight goods
  • Emergency recovery after ocean delay

❌ When air may be wrong

  • Lead time is flexible
  • Goal is reducing spend
  • Dense, non-urgent, commercially tolerant shipment
"The right mode depends on the cost of lateness, not just the freight invoice."

✈ Module 3 of 10
Air export: how freight physically moves

Export flow — step by step

A salesperson must understand the flow well enough to qualify correctly, avoid impossible promises, and explain timing honestly.

  1. Commercial qualification — origin, destination, commodity, dimensions, weight, Incoterms, cargo-ready date, must-arrive date
  2. Rate sourcing and service design — direct vs connecting, express vs deferred, special handling
  3. Booking — capacity request and confirmation
  4. Pickup and origin handling — collection, dimension check, packaging confirmation
  5. Export documentation — invoice, packing list, air waybill data, security data
  6. Terminal acceptance — weigh, measure, screen, check acceptance criteria
  7. Build-up and uplift — cargo moves in ULDs or loose load for flight departure
Most preventable delays happen before departure: bad docs, wrong dimensions, cargo not ready, screening issues, or missed cutoffs.

✈ Module 4 of 10
Air import: what happens after the flight lands

Import flow

Many salespeople oversell departure and undersell arrival. Customers often care more about release and delivery than the flight itself.

  1. Pre-alert — destination team receives shipment data and documents in advance
  2. Arrival — cargo lands and is transferred into terminal custody
  3. Breakdown — ULDs are broken down for release processing
  4. Import process — customs filing, compliance review, duties/taxes handling
  5. Deconsolidation — freight is separated by shipment if applicable
  6. Final delivery — trucking or courier to consignee if included in scope
Arrival is not the same as release. A landed shipment may still be held due to customs, terminal processing, payment, or document issues.

✈ Module 5 of 10
Pricing: actual, volumetric, and chargeable weight

Key definitions

Actual weight

Physical scale weight of the cargo.

Volumetric weight

A pricing representation of space consumed by the cargo.

Chargeable weight

Whichever is higher — actual or volumetric. This is what you are billed on.

A light but bulky shipment can rate higher than a heavier but denser one. Offer alternatives: denser packing, deferred air, consolidation, or mode shift when timing allows.

✈ Module 6 of 10
Scope, Incoterms, and the difference between a good quote and a bad one

What a strong quote clarifies

Many price objections are actually scope misunderstandings. "An air quote" could mean airport-to-airport, airport-to-door, or full door-to-door service.

  • Pickup included or excluded
  • Final delivery included or excluded
  • Customs included or excluded
  • Duties and taxes included or excluded
  • Dimensions, commodity, and validity assumptions
  • Transit expectation and service level
Incoterms matter because they define commercial responsibility and help prevent quoting the wrong scope.

Operations literacy for salespeople

What sales should understand

  • Core documents: invoice, packing list, air waybill data, and any permits
  • Customs risk triggers: vague descriptions, inconsistent values, wrong consignee data, uncertain classification
  • Handling risks: dangerous goods, batteries, oversized cargo, fragile cargo, temperature-sensitive freight
  • How to communicate exceptions: what happened, what is being done, when the next update will be sent
Post-sale communication is part of pre-sale reputation.

Traditional vs digital air freight sales

What digital actually means

Digital sales does not merely mean using software. It means using software and process discipline to make buying faster, clearer, more transparent, more accurate, and easier to execute.

Traditional sales

  • Email and phone-heavy quoting
  • Reactive service
  • Limited structured discovery
  • Price-first communication
  • Inconsistent handoff quality

Digital sales

  • Structured intake and discovery
  • Clear assumptions, visible milestones
  • Consultative option design
  • Faster, repeatable quoting workflows
  • Stronger promise-to-execution alignment
The real differentiator is decision quality at speed.

✈ Module 9 of 10
Objection handling for air freight salespeople

Core principles

Most objections are not objections to air freight itself. They are objections to incomplete understanding, price shock, unclear scope, or poor comparison.

  • Explain before discounting
  • Check scope equivalence before reacting to competitor pricing
  • Translate freight cost into business impact
  • Offer alternatives instead of defending one option emotionally
"Let's make sure we're comparing the right thing. Does the competitor's price include pickup, customs coordination, destination handling, and final delivery — or is it airport-to-airport only?"

✈ Module 10 of 10
Discovery framework and sales scripts

Five-question discovery framework

A strong discovery sequence establishes the facts needed to produce a usable quote rather than a placeholder number.

  1. What is the commodity?
  2. What are the exact dimensions, weight, and piece count?
  3. When is cargo ready, and when must it arrive?
  4. What scope is needed — airport-only or full door service?
  5. What are the Incoterms, and who controls the freight?
"To recommend the right air service and not just send a placeholder number, I need commodity, dimensions, cargo-ready date, required arrival date, and whether you need airport-only or full door delivery included."
